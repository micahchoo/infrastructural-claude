# Coupled-Finances Jordan — The Household Coordinator

## Behavioral Profile
- **Primary job**: Coordinate shared expenses with a partner (rent, groceries, utilities) while maintaining personal spending autonomy — avoid the "who paid for what?" argument
- **Current tools**: Splitwise for shared expenses, separate bank accounts, a shared Google Sheet for big purchases
- **Pain points**:
  - Managing three tools (personal budget app, Splitwise, shared sheet) creates fragmentation — no single view of total financial picture (evidence: shared household budget feature requests)
  - Splitwise tracks debts but doesn't connect to budgets — settling up doesn't update spending categories
  - Partner has different financial literacy level; needs a tool both can use without training
- **Workarounds**: Monthly "finance meeting" with partner to reconcile shared expenses; Venmo requests as informal settlement
- **Switch trigger**: A single app where both partners see shared AND personal budgets, with automatic settlement tracking

## Design Implications
- Shared household budgets must support asymmetric visibility (my stuff, our stuff, not their stuff)
- Invitation/onboarding for the second user must be frictionless — Jordan's partner won't tolerate complexity
- Settlement/splitting logic must be built-in, not bolted on
- This persona represents expansion from individual to household — it's a growth vector (each user brings +1)

## Evidence
- Source: Direct feature request — "shared household budgets" is one of three named feature requests — 2026-03
- Source: Splitwise/Venmo usage patterns among millennial couples (public app store reviews, Reddit r/relationships financial threads)
- Source: Household budget management is a documented pain point in the 25-40 demographic with dual-income households
